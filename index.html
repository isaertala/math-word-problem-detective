🗝️ Keyword Clue Bank

+ Addition Clues

These words mean put together, so ADD.

altogether in all total sum combined in total both
Example: Sam has 4 red balloons and 5 blue balloons. How many balloons does he have in total?
4 + 5 = 9 balloons

− Subtraction Clues

These words mean take away or compare, so SUBTRACT.

left fewer difference how many more remain gave away less than
Example: There are 10 birds on a branch. 4 birds fly away. How many birds are left?
10 − 4 = 6 birds

× Multiplication Clues

These words mean equal groups joining, so MULTIPLY.

each every per times groups of twice double
Example: There are 4 pages of stickers. Each page has 3 stickers. How many stickers in all?
4 × 3 = 12 stickers

÷ Division Clues

These words mean splitting into equal groups, so DIVIDE.

share equally split each group divided by equally among how many in each
Example: 15 cookies are shared equally among 5 friends. How many cookies does each friend get?
15 ÷ 5 = 3 cookies

⚠ Watch Out! Two-Step Clues

Sometimes a keyword means there's a HIDDEN step. You must work out one thing FIRST, then use that answer to solve the real question.

more than less than than after then combined age left after buying both
Example: Tom has 5 apples. Mia has 3 more apples than Tom. How many apples do they have altogether?
Step 1 (hidden step): Mia's apples = 5 + 3 = 8
Step 2 (real question): 5 + 8 = 13 apples altogether

📦 Container Clues

Watch for boxes, baskets, bags, jars, or groups holding equal amounts — they're often a multiplication or division clue! If you know how many are in EACH container and how many containers, MULTIPLY. If you know the TOTAL and how many containers, DIVIDE to find how many in each.

box / boxes basket / baskets bag / bags jar / jars container group / groups package / pack crate
Example: There are 3 baskets. Each basket has 6 oranges. How many oranges are there in total?
You know the amount in EACH basket, so MULTIPLY: 3 × 6 = 18 oranges
🕵️ A detective's reminder: keywords are helpful clues, but they're not a magic trick! Sometimes a word like "more" can still mean subtraction, depending on the story. Always read the WHOLE problem and think about what is actually happening — the keyword just gives you a hint, it doesn't decide the answer for you.

CASE FILE #2

Liam has 6 toy cars. His brother gives him 5 more toy cars. How many toy cars does Liam have in all?

The keyword is "in all" — that means put the groups together. Which operation is that?
6 + 5 = 11. Liam has 11 toy cars in all. CASE CLOSED

CASE FILE #3

There are 14 birds on a fence. 9 birds fly away. How many birds are left on the fence?

The keyword is "left" — some birds flew away, so the group got smaller.
14 − 9 = 5. There are 5 birds left on the fence. CASE CLOSED

CASE FILE #4

Ana picked 7 red apples and 8 green apples. How many apples did she pick altogether?

The keyword is "altogether" — two groups of apples are joining together.
7 + 8 = 15. Ana picked 15 apples altogether. CASE CLOSED

CASE FILE #5

Each shelf has 5 books. There are 3 shelves. How many books are there in all?

The keyword is "Each" — equal groups joining together means MULTIPLY.
5 × 3 = 15. There are 15 books in all. CASE CLOSED

CASE FILE #6

18 marbles are shared equally among 3 friends. How many marbles does each friend get?

The keyword is "shared equally" — splitting into equal groups means DIVIDE.
18 ÷ 3 = 6. Each friend gets 6 marbles. CASE CLOSED

CASE FILE #7

Jack has 6 stamps. Mia has 4 more stamps than Jack. How many stamps do they have altogether?

The keyword is "more than" — that's a hidden step! First find Mia's stamps, THEN add both.
Step 1: Mia's stamps = 6 + 4 = 10. Step 2: 6 + 10 = 16. They have 16 stamps altogether. CASE CLOSED

CASE FILE #8

There are 4 boxes. Each box has 7 pencils. How many pencils are there in total?

The keywords are "boxes" and "Each" — you know the amount in each container, so MULTIPLY.
4 × 7 = 28. There are 28 pencils in total. CASE CLOSED

📜 Olympiad Rules

The official rules for competing in the yearly Olympiad — please read carefully.

  • 🪪 Eligibility: A free account is required, and you choose your Age Group when you sign up (Ages 3–8, 9–11, 12–15, 16–18, or 19+ with no upper limit). Your age group sets your division — you can compete in your own division or any harder one above it, but never an easier one below it. Grand Master Division (19+) has no age ceiling — geeks and nerds are welcome to compete forever! 🤓
  • 📝 Sign-Up: Registration opens in June, ahead of the July–November exam season, so you can create your account and get ready before your division's month arrives.
  • 💻 Format: The Olympiad exam is taken entirely online, from any device with internet access — no testing center or physical attendance required.
  • 📋 Questions: Each division's Olympiad exam has at least 100 word-problem cases — some divisions or years may include more.
  • ⏱️ Time: There's no hard time limit — solve at your own pace. Your total completion time is recorded down to the second.
  • 🕒 Tie-Breakers: Completion time is a secondary ranking factor. If two or more participants earn the same score in a division, whoever finished with the faster completion time ranks higher.
  • 🔁 Attempts: One official attempt per division, per year — once submitted, there are no retakes.
  • 🎖️ Scoring Tiers: Gold Detective (95%+), Silver Detective (90%+), Bronze Detective (85%+), Rookie Detective (80%+). Below 80%, participants receive a Certificate of Participation instead of a scoring tier.
  • 📜 Everyone Gets a Certificate: Every participant receives a free digital certificate — a Certificate of Achievement with a scoring tier for 80%+, or a Certificate of Participation for everyone else.
  • 🏆 Winners & Announcements: Official National Top 10 winners are announced at least 7 days after a division's exam month ends, giving time to review any scoring disputes. Once that 7-day window closes, rankings are final and can no longer be disputed.
  • 🎁 Prizes: The National Top 10 earn a merch prize pack; the Top 3 also receive medals 🥇🥈🥉. Merch items are based on availability and are not guaranteed to be the exact items shown.
  • 💳 Fees: Any Olympiad registration fees are non-refundable, including in cases of missed deadlines, disqualification, or withdrawal.
  • 📅 Scheduling: Each division's exam window is open for its entire assigned month only — check the Schedule tab for exact dates.
  • 🔄 Subject to Change: Divisions, question counts, scoring tiers, prizes, and all other rules are subject to change from year to year.

🧠 Real Talk

Why Master Word Problems?

Because every job on Earth is really just word problems in disguise.

A word problem isn't just a school thing. It's a story with a question hiding inside it — and reading past the story to find the real question is a skill that never stops being useful. Doctors, engineers, chefs, astronauts, and CEOs all solve word problems every single day. They just don't call them that anymore — they call it "the job."

🌟 Even the smartest people in the world deal with word problems

The world's top scientists work out how much fuel a rocket needs. The world's top CEOs work out whether a business idea will make or lose money. World leaders work out how to feed millions of people with a limited budget. None of that is possible without the exact same skill you're building right here with the CLUES method: read carefully, find the real question, and choose the right operation. Every case you solve is practice for a lifetime of solving them — the numbers just get bigger.
